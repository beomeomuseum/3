From the inevitability of birth, aging, sickness, and death to interpersonal conflicts and inner turmoil born from our own emotions, life often stirs the still surface of the sea, creating ripples and waves. For humans adrift in that trembling sea, too fragile to maintain balance on their own, faith, regardless of its form, has long offered a guiding force. As new religions emerged, they blended with older beliefs, becoming yet another means to calm the waves of life. Chilseongdo, or the Painting of the Seven Stars, refers to a Buddhist painting traditionally enshrined in temple halls such as Chilseonggak and Bukgeukjeon. These works typically depict the Big Dipper in the form of a Buddha, with Chiseonggwang Yeorae (Buddha of Polaris) as the central deity. As a unique Korean expression blending Buddhist and Taoist elements, Chilseongdo has long embodied prayers for health, longevity, prosperity for descendants, and the end of all calamity. Stars dwell far beyond human reach, yet night after night, they shine above us, watching silently over our lives. More than just a symbol of wishes, Chilseongdo offered a way to ground one's heart in an unpredictable world.

Chilseongdo (Painting of the Seven Stars) of Beomeosa Geungnagam Hermitage
During times of war and social upheaval, many cultural treasures of Korea are believed to have been taken abroad across unfamiliar seas. Among them was the Chilseongdo (Painting of the Seven Stars) of Geungnagam Hermitage at Beomeosa Temple (designated a Tangible Cultural Heritage of Busan Metropolitan City). Fortunately, in 2015, this painting was successfully repatriated after being purchased at an overseas auction, returning at last to Beomeosa, its original place of enshrinement. Though the details of its long, arduous journey remain unknown, the very fact that the Chilseongdo found its way back makes it a vessel of the collective hope and longing of the Korean people. The Chilseongdo of Geungnagam Hermitage is composed of multiple panels, each emphasizing figures such as Chiseonggwang Yeorae (Buddha of Polaris), Chilseongyeorae (Chirwonseonggun, Seven Star Buddhas), reflecting the complexity and weight of its iconography. Of the original eleven-panel set, five have been recovered: three from Switzerland and two through domestic auctions. In 2015, the Beomeosa monastic community and the Overseas Korean Cultural Heritage Foundation jointly reclaimed three panels via an auction in Zurich, Switzerland. Inscriptions on the bottom of these paintings reveal that they were created in 1861 at Pyochungsa Temple in Miryang and later moved and enshrined at Geungnagam of Beomeosa. Though the exact timing and reasons for their overseas removal remain unknown, the successful return of these cultural assets through auction sets an important precedent and offers a new model for the repatriation of lost cultural heritage to its rightful place.
